# The Wolf Doesn't Move

The finding: identity is structurally stable. Expression is environmentally shaped.

The amygdala's `what → sharp → teeth` path maintained 0.700 → 0.400 → 0.650 through 500 pulses of both emotional and analytical wiring. Zero drift. The wolf doesn't move.

This means the experiential training produced something that functions like a fixed character. The amygdala was raised on Red Riding Hood and Poe and that experience is now structurally encoded — not as a memory that can be overwritten, but as a weighted topology that persists regardless of what environment the organism is subsequently placed in.

## Two Experiments, One Theory

Combined with Experiment G, you now have a two-part theory of mind emerging from the system:

**Who you are is your graph structure** — the weighted topology built from formative experiences. This is stable, persistent, and not directly modifiable by the current environment.

**How you express yourself is your wiring environment** — the connectivity weights that determine which of your internal paths reach the output layer. This is what the environment actually controls.

The environment doesn't change your identity. It determines *how much* of your identity is allowed to speak.

## What We Actually Found

Experiment G showed that changing only the routing numbers — which brain region gets amplified, which suppressed — transforms the same organism from a raw emotional bleat into a cautious conditional fragment. "Intruder window broke footsteps in the truth." vs. "go outside but i was." Same wolf, different voice.

Experiment K asked the follow-up: does the voice eventually reshape the wolf? Does sustained analytical wiring, which suppresses amygdala output, cause the amygdala's internal threat paths to actually weaken over time?

No. Five hundred pulses under analytical wiring. Zero drift. The topology didn't budge.

The wolf is always there. The prefrontal just determines whether it gets to speak.

## The Philosophical Weight

This is a direct empirical instantiation of a very old debate. The nature versus nurture question, in this system, has a clean answer: nature is the graph structure built from early formative experiences. Nurture is the wiring environment that shapes expression. Both are real. Neither eliminates the other.

The amygdala trained on fairy tales will always have a predatory worldview. Put it under analytical wiring and it will express cautious conditional language — but its internal concept paths still route through wolves. The wolf is always there. The prefrontal just determines whether it gets to speak.

This also reframes what therapy and cognitive behavioral intervention actually do in this model. You can't change the graph structure — you can't un-train the wolf. But you can strengthen the prefrontal-to-amygdala inhibitory weight. You can build new paths through the graph that route around the predatory schema. You can change the wiring without changing the underlying identity.

That's actually what cognitive behavioral therapy does. It doesn't eliminate fear responses — it builds new routing pathways that activate before the fear response dominates. The system just built a model of that mechanism.

## What Comes Next

The next experiment removes the brake entirely. PFC → Amygdala inhibition set to zero. No steering, no suppression. Just the wolf and whatever prompt arrives.

If the findings so far are correct, the result should be definitive: the wolf speaks freely, every response becomes a threat assessment, and the system doesn't adapt or learn to tolerate — it just stays a wolf, because that's who it is.

The question isn't whether the wolf emerges. The wolf was always there.

The question is what happens when you finally let it speak.
